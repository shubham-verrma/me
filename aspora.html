Product-Led Growth Fintech NRI Banking Remittances UAE · UK

Aspora was solving one of the most under-served problems in fintech: banking for the global Indian diaspora. Millions of NRIs across UAE, UK, and North America were stuck with legacy accounts, punishing remittance fees, and products not designed for their cross-border lives. Aspora built the neobank they deserved.

I joined in 2024 as part of the Growth and PLG function. The mandate was clear — build the pipeline for Aspora's new NRI banking product before the full launch. That meant understanding who the early adopter was, where they congregated, and how to get them into a waitlist that would create natural momentum at launch.

I designed and deployed the PLG waitlist system: positioning frameworks, referral mechanics, onboarding flows, and the content strategy that spoke directly to NRI pain points. The approach was research-first — I ran user interviews, mapped remittance journeys, and identified the exact friction points that made the product a compelling switch.

Over six months, the growth systems I built helped Aspora enhance over $10 million in remittance volume across the UAE and UK corridors. More importantly, it validated the activation loop that would power their full-scale launch — and confirmed Aspora as one of India's fastest-growing fintech startups.

What I Learned

PLG in a
trust-first category.

01

Trust is the first product feature in fintech

Before any growth lever works, the user has to trust the product with their money. Every PLG mechanic needs to be designed with credibility and safety signals baked in — growth without trust inverts immediately.

02

Diaspora communities amplify through identity, not incentives

The most effective referral mechanics for NRI audiences weren't discount-driven — they were identity-driven. Helping someone "send more money home" resonated 10x more than a cashback offer.

03

Waitlists only work when they create real FOMO

A waitlist without scarcity, community, or progress signals is just a delay. The mechanics that worked best were ones that made users feel they were joining something exclusive and early — not waiting in a queue.

04

Market corridors have radically different activation paths

UAE and UK NRIs behave completely differently — different remittance frequency, different trust anchors, different channels. What worked in Dubai flopped in London. Localisation is not optional in diaspora fintech.
